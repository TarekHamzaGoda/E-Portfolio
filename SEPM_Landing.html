Discussion 1: Causes Of Project failures

According to (Lehtinen, et al., 2013) the common cause of project failure is derived from the following categories:

  • 1. People: Factors related to inexperience.
  • 2. Tasks: Factors related to task complexity and output.
  • 3. Methods: Factors related to project processes and monitoring
  • 4. Environment: Factor related to resources, and schedules.

    • Furthermore, I believe that the three most common cause of project failure are the following:


  • Inadequate Planning and Scope Management: Undefined objectives can lead to confusion and constant change in requirements. As a result, an Inaccurate resource estimation may occur which increases the risks of budget overruns and missed deadlines.
  • Ineffective Collaboration and Communication: Communication breakdowns between stakeholders, team members, and management can result in conflicts. The lack of clear communication can affect decision making and will lead to redundancy, reduced productivity and wasted efforts.
  • Poor Risk Management: Failing to assess and identify potential risks at the beginning phases of the project can lead to unexpected delays. This will neglect to develop a mitigation plan that may result in risk escalations and serious issues. Moreover, ignoring external factors such as advancements in technology and changes in market needs can leave the project subject to disruptions.

    • Below are examples of project failures as result of the causes mentioned above:


  • Healthcare.gov (2013). A platform for health insurance plans. The platformed suffered from slow loading times, frequent crashes and incorrect data processing. this came as a result of inadequate planning considering that the project had an ambitious scope within a tight deadline (Anthopoulos, et al., 2016).
  • UK National Programme for IT: An initiative to digitize the healthcare IT system in the UK. Due to the scope expanding over time and the increase of the project complexity created difficulties in managing the requirements leading to budget overrun that eventually resulted in a financial strain (Justinia, 2016).

    Unit 5 Discussion: Factors Affecting User Experience

    Figure 1 in Minge & Thuring (2018) paper illustrates a CUE model that indicates the emotional reactions based on user perception of a system. In particular, the CUE model seems to highlight the initial user interactions by mainly capturing it’s influence by the usability and visual aesthetics. However, the full adaptation of this model might be redundant considering that this approach might not contribute to long term user satisfaction and loyalty.

    A UX framework that takes into account the evolving nature of user experiences over time might be crucial for creating a user-centric product. According to (Karapanos, et al., 2011), few strategies can be implemented that can incorporate the dynamics of user emotions into a UX framework:

  • User Research:Conducting comprehensive research to understand the target audience, hence mapping the emotional response through different stages of the interaction with the product. This includes finding information on the user’s motivations and expectations when interacting with the system.
  • User Journey:Development of detailed user journey maps that illustrates the emotional states users will encounter during their first use and those that become more experienced.
  • User onboarding:Ensuring that the user’s expectations are managed and that the user is guided through the features of the system at the first glance. In addition, ensuring that the interface is user-friendly to avoid potential frustration and confusion.
  • Personalization:Personalized features tailored based individual preferences ensure controlled emotional response.

    • By integrating these strategies into the UX framework, you can create a more adaptable and user-centered design that accommodates the changing emotional landscape of users from their initial interaction to becoming more experienced with the product.

    Dicussion Summery: Factors Affecting User Experience

    Managing user reactions from a software and UX perspective and as a Software Project Manager mainly involves addressing user functionality and interface design. According to Mahlke and Thuring (2007) acceptance model for user experience, technology acceptance was determined by variables such as perceived usefulness and perceived ease of use. Additionally, characteristics such as terminology, navigation and screen design played a critical role in the over satisfaction of users. Managing user reactions in this case involves a combination of proactive strategies, continuous improvement, and effective communication. Shengli Deng and Yuling Fang (2015) Provides a guide that can assist in managing user emotional experience:

  • Establishing a Communication Channel: Ensures the accessibility to user feedback and concerns. This can be implemented by providing user-friendly support systems, encouraging user to share feedback on their experience through surveys and forums.
  • Active Listing & Feedback Analysis: Listening to user concerns while analysing and identifying patterns and common issues. Implementation includes conducting regular reviews of user feedback and tracking user behaviour (Christian & Malone , 2009).
  • Regular Updates & Continuous Improvements: Keeping user informed of new features, improvements and bug fixes. This can be implemented by highlighting user feedback influence and prioritizing user feedback in development sprints.
  • Setting Realistic Expectations: User expectations about software limitations and capabilities should be manged by clearly communicating scope, realistic timelines and feature implementations.

    • User emotional reactions play a significant role in the success of any software product. As such, project managers must take into account these guidelines to ensure the maximum success of any UX development especially when it comes to functionality and interface design. Therefore, the effects of user emotional reactions can be determined in these areas (Hartson & Pyla , n.d.):

  • User Engagement and Satisfaction: While users that have positive reaction towards a software tend to engage actively and perceive it as valuable, Negative reactions can lead to negative reviews and in extreme cases, abandonment.
  • Brand Perception: Users associate a positive experience with the brand as a whole. However, frustration resulting from negative experience can result in overall brand dissatisfaction that can affect the trust and loyalty.

    • In conclusion. addressing user functionality and interface design with a focus on eliciting positive emotional reactions is crucial for the overall success and sustainability of a software product. As a project Manager, implementing these strategies can actively control user emotions.

    E-Portfolio Activity: Scope Creep

    Scope creep is the gradual expansion of modification of a project’s initial objectives without the any approval. This occurs when new functionalities are added without any corresponding increase in time constraints, budget or resources. Eventually, scope creeping can lead to project delays, cost overruns.

    What aspects of a project are dependent on scope creep?

  • Timeline: changes in objectives can lead to delays and extended timeline
  • Budget: Increased resource requirement leading to budget overruns
  • Quality: Rushed implementation as result of scope creeping can compromise the overall quality

    • How to balance the competing requirements when scope creep occurs?

    There are several strategies to for balancing requirements during scope creep that includes:

  • Prioritization: Defining most critical features that meets project objectives
  • Impact Assessment: Evaluate the impact of proposed changes on timeline and budget.
  • Trade-offs: Identify area where new feature can replace agreed features.

    • How do you stop scope creep?

  • Comprehensive Planning: Invest time in thorough project planning. Clearly define project objectives, requirements, and deliverables upfront.
  • Regular Monitoring: Continuously monitor the project against the original plan. If deviations occur, address them promptly and involve stakeholders in decision-making.
  • Educate Stakeholders: Ensure that all stakeholders understand the potential consequences of scope changes, including impacts on timeline, budget, and project quality.

    E-Portfolio Activity: Software Quality Factors

    Software quality factors has evolved significantly since the 1970’s as a result of newer considerations and technological developments. This contributed significantly to the software development standards in many areas. In an article by (Lee, 2014) that discusses current software quality metrics, several factors are compared against McCall’s paper (McCall et al., 1977) during the 1970’s:

    Complexity and Scale:

  • 1970s: Software projects were often smaller in scale and less complex. The systems developed were typically standalone applications, and the development processes were less formalized.
  • 1970s: Software projects were often smaller in scale and less complex. The systems developed were typically standalone applications, and the development processes were less formalized.

    • Development Methodologies:

  • 1970s: Waterfall model and other traditional, linear methodologies were commonly used. Development was often a sequential process with little room for iteration.
  • Today: Agile methodologies, DevOps, and continuous integration are widespread. Iterative and incremental development processes allow for more flexibility, adaptability to changing requirements, and continuous improvement.

    • 3. Security:

  • 1970s: Security concerns were less prominent, and there was a limited awareness of potential vulnerabilities. Security measures were often an afterthought.
  • Today: Security is a top priority. Software development practices include security considerations from the early stages, and there is a focus on implementing best practices to protect against cyber threats and vulnerabilities.

    • Availability and Reliability:

  • 1970s: System availability and reliability were important but were sometimes compromised due to hardware limitations and less robust software design.
  • Today: High availability and reliability are critical, especially in mission-critical systems and cloud-based applications. Redundancy, fault tolerance, and disaster recovery planning are common practices.
